Cartel stability¶

Part I: Description of the data¶

source: online addotional material of Stock, J.H. and Watson, M.W. (2007). Introduction to Econometrics, 2nd ed. Boston: Addison Wesley. Formerly in Porter, R. H. (1983). A Study of Cartel Stability: The Joint Executive Committee, 1880–1886. The Bell Journal of Economics, 14, 301–314.

Description of the data: They are weekly observations, from 1880 to 1886, on prices for transportation of grain in the Great Lakes region in the US, where transportation could be made by train or by boat depending on the weather conditions.

The observation are made on 326 weeks which is the population (and sample) under study. There are 5 variables:

  • price (numerical discrete): weekly index of price of shipping a ton of grain by rail (7 levels)
  • cartel (categorical): Existence of an operative railroad cartel.
  • quantity (continuous): total tonnage of grain shipped in the week. From 4,800 to 77,000 approximatively.

_ season (categorical): indicating season of year. To match the weekly data, the calendar has been divided into 13 periods, each approximately 4 weeks long.

  • ice (categorical): Indicates whether the Great Lakes innavigable because of ice.

Objective: The data were gathered mainly to understand whether the presence of a railway Cartel would influence the level of prices.

In [25]:

Since the sample is large enough ($n=328$), the confidence interval is $(p0-t, p0+t)$ where the margin of error $t$ is given by $$t = u_{1-\alpha/2}\frac{\sqrt{p0(1-p0)}}{n}$$ where $\alpha = 1-0.95$ and $u_{1-\alpha/2}$ is the $1-\alpha/2$ quantile of the $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ distribution:

In [26]:
alpha = 0.05
u = st.norm.ppf(1-alpha/2)
t = u * np.sqrt(p0*(1-p0)/n)
print(f'the proportion of weeks with ice at the Great Lakes is in ({p0-t:.2f},{p0+t:.2f}) with confidence level 95%')

the proportion of weeks with ice at the Great Lakes is in (0.37,0.48) with confidence level 95%

Part IV: Goodness-of-fit test¶

Nota bene: when dealing with some distribution that you do not know but have identified, you must be able to estimate its parameters. Usually, this is given on Wikipedia (or of course in Stitistics books). You always have to write a word about the estimation of the parameters. More over, you have to estimate the parameter that are used in scipy.stats for the theoretical distribution you are comparing your distribution to, not others.

Fitting the quantity to a Gamma distribution¶

I estimate the parameters by the method of moments:

$$\hat\beta = \frac{\overline X}{S^2},\quad \hat\alpha = \hat\beta\; \overline X.$$

Chi-squared value and test¶

The actal normalized distance between the to distibutions has approximately a chi-squared distribution with $J-1-2$ degress of freedom under the assumption $H_0$ of adequation of distributions. The $p$-value of the test is the probability of having at least such distance under the assumption $H_0$. We make a test at a significance level of $\alpha =$10%

In [35]:
di = (ni-npi)**2/npi
d = np.sum(di)
pvalue = st.chi2.sf(d, J-1-2)
pvalue
Out[35]:
0.6326299364074179

The $p$-value is far grater than $\alpha$. This means that there is no significant prove to reject $H_0$ and we consider that the fit is correct.

A parametric test to compare the means of two samples¶

Here the two samples are not Gaussian, therefore we cannot compare the variance with a Fisher test but we cal still compare the mean.
